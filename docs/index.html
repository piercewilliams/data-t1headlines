Finding 1 · Apple News Formulas

Key finding: No formula shows statistically confirmed lift above baseline—but several significantly drag performance below it. "Here's / Here are" posts the highest median percentile (59%) on only n=17 articles—directional, not confirmed. Number leads and question-format headlines statistically underperform the baseline (padj=0.002 ** and padj<0.001 *** respectively, BH-adj). The formula alone isn't the signal—how you execute it is.

Across 3,138 non-Featured articles, three formula types significantly underperform the baseline: number leads (0.83×, padj=0.002 **), question format (0.65×, padj<0.001 ***), and quoted ledes (0.63×, padj<0.001 ***)—all BH-FDR adjusted. The better-performing formulas—"Here's / Here are" (1.26×) and possessive named entity (1.19×)—are directional but not statistically significant at current sample sizes (n=17 and n=103 respectively).

Finding 1b · Number Leads: Deep Dive

Bottom line: "127 arrested" outperforms "100 arrested," and "15 takeaways" outperforms "50 things to know."

Finding 2 · Featured on Apple News

Key tension: "What to know" gets featured by Apple at 1.81× the baseline rate—and non-featured WTN articles trend toward the lower end of the distribution. This is directional and not significant at α=0.05 (padj=0.12, n=16 non-featured WTN articles)—interpret with caution. The Featured signal is statistically robust; the organic underperformance is a pattern worth watching, not a confirmed finding. Use WTN specifically when chasing Featured placement; avoid applying it as a general-purpose formula until the organic performance data strengthens.

Among the 1036 Featured articles in our dataset, "What to know" headlines are dramatically overrepresented: 13 of 29 (45%) were Featured, versus 24.8% overall. This is a statistically robust formula signal (χ²=5.2, padj=0.03 *).

Question-format headlines are also Featured more often than expected (41%, 1.64× lift, padj<0.001 ***)—but they significantly underperform other Featured articles once selected. Apple's editors favor questions; the format itself doesn't follow through on views.

Quoted ledes present the inverse pattern: Featured at roughly the baseline rate (34%), but once Featured they deliver among the highest within-Featured percentiles. Questions get into the Featured tier and stall; quoted ledes get in and overperform.

Causal note: The association between "What to know" and Featured placement is observational. The causal direction is ambiguous: editors may independently choose the same stories that writers frame as "What to know," rather than the format itself driving featuring.

Finding 3 · SmartNews Allocation

The channel allocation is inverted: the highest-performing channels are starved while the lowest-performing channel dominates volume. No new content required—better channel framing captures the gains.

SmartNews category channel data reveals a severe allocation mismatch. Articles appearing in the Local channel sit at the 97%ile of their monthly cohort (16,593 median raw views). Finding 4 · Push Notifications

Two signals dominate: "Exclusive" tag (2.49× CTR lift, padj<0.001 ***) and named person + possessive construction (1.86×, padj<0.001 ***). The counter-intuitive result: short notifications (≤80 chars) get 39% fewer clicks. Longer, more descriptive notification text outperforms across the board.

Across 351 Apple News push notifications (Jan–Feb 2026, median CTR 1.68%), 5 features show statistically significant effects after FDR correction. The "exclusive" tag is the strongest at 2.49× lift. The possessive framing signal: notifications with a full named person AND a possessive construction drive 1.86× CTR vs. 1.21× for merely naming someone. Question format hurts at 0.64×, consistent with the Apple News article finding.

Sensitivity analysis: "Exclusive" with and without Guthrie cluster

The 16 "exclusive"-tagged notifications include 4 that also mention Guthrie. Removing the Guthrie overlap: the "exclusive" lift falls from 2.49× to 2.07× (p=0.002 **). The effect remains statistically significant—the exclusive signal is not solely a Guthrie artifact.

The serial/escalating story as a content type

The top 10 notifications by CTR are dominated by a single ongoing story: Nancy Guthrie's disappearance and its connection to Savannah Guthrie. This defines a content type: the serial/escalating story with a celebrity anchor. The formula: possessive named entity + new development + escalating stakes, published in installments. The structural recipe: "[Celebrity]'s [family member/associate] [new disclosure/development]."

What doesn't move the needle: neither "contains a number" (n=87, 1.13×) nor "attribution"—says/told/reports (n=30, 1.08×)—survive FDR correction.

Apple News Notifications, Jan–Feb 2026 (n=351 with valid CTR). Mann-Whitney U; effect size = rank-biserial r; 95% CIs via 1,000-iteration bootstrap. BH–FDR across all 7 feature tests. N=2 months only—findings are directional. Feature classifier unvalidated.

Finding 5 · Platform Topic Inversion

Action: Write platform-specific variant briefs for sports and nature/wildlife—these two categories show the strongest inversions. Apple News sports: lead with team/player + outcome. SmartNews sports: don't rely on sports for reach—use local/civic and breaking news instead. Nature/wildlife is the mirror: underperforms on Apple News (0.73× platform median) but outperforms on SmartNews (1.21×).

Sports ranks #2 on Apple News (percentile index 1.12× platform median) but #9—last—on SmartNews (index 0.77×). This is not a small difference: sports sits well above the Apple News median and well below the SmartNews median. The inversion is statistically significant (Mann-Whitney U, p<0.001 ***) across the full year of 2025 data.

Nature/wildlife shows the reverse: it underperforms the Apple News median (0.73×) but outperforms the SmartNews median (1.21×). Among the top 30 most frequent words in top-quartile headlines on each platform, only 8 words appear on both lists (cops, death, found, photos, say, shares, star, trump)—generic reporting terms rather than shared topical vocabulary, suggesting the platforms reward very different content angles.

Finding 6 · Headline Variance by Topic

Action: Concentrate variant production on high-variance topics—business (IQR/median = 1.55) and lifestyle (1.56) on Apple News—where the gap between a top-quartile and bottom-quartile headline is widest. Crime and sports are more consistent mid-performers with less room to move.

The chart shows IQR ÷ median of percentile_within_cohort for each topic × platform. A ratio of 1.5 means the articles between the 25th and 75th percentile span 1.5× the median—a wide, unpredictable range. Where this ratio is high, headline choice has the most room to lift or drag performance.

Read this chart as: IQR ÷ median of percentile rank—a scale-free measure of outcome spread. A higher value means the gap between a 25th-percentile and 75th-percentile article is wider relative to the median: more variance, more room for headline choice to make a difference. Lower values (sports, weather) mean outcomes cluster tightly—less leverage from headline optimization alone.

Finding 7 · Views vs. Reading Depth

Action: Don't use view count as the sole ROI signal for variant allocation. A variant driving 5,000 views at 75s average active time may deliver more subscriber retention value than one driving 20,000 views at 45s. The model should incorporate views (reach), saves (return intent), and active time (read depth)—all three are available in this dataset.

The Apple News dataset includes both Total Views and average active time per article. Pearson r = -0.010 (p = 0.54), Spearman ρ = -0.030 (p = 0.05). Both agree: views and reading time show near-zero correlation across 4,174 articles—high-reach and deep-read articles are largely distinct populations. The view count spans a 546× range across deciles; active time moves only 4 seconds.

MetricCorrelation with Total ViewsWhat it measures
Avg. active timer = -0.010, ρ = -0.030 (not significant)Depth of the current read
Savesr = 0.82 (strong)Intent to return / bookmark behavior
Likesr = 0.77 (strong)Affirmation / social signal
Article sharesr = 0.67 (strong)Distribution / word of mouth

Featured articles illustrate this split directly: 3.1× median view lift, but only 51s active time vs. 56s for non-Featured (p<0.0001). Apple's editorial promotion drives discovery; readers who arrive via Featured spend slightly less time than readers who actively sought the article. Separately, subscribers average 49s active time vs. 55s for non-subscribers—likely a usage behavior difference (subscribers browse more, read less per article) rather than a content quality gap.

Apple News 2025–2026 (n=4,174 articles with valid active time). 30 articles have active time <10s; 3 have >300s—not filtered, ~1% of records. Spearman ρ is the preferred test for independence given skewed views distribution.
