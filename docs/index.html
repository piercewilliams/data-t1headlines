Context · The Frame Deck

What the March 24 external analysis got right — and where it stops short

The Justin Frame executive deck (circulated March 24, 2026) is a credible piece of work on a narrow question: which article types and headline formulas drive the most page views in a specific dataset. The headline formula findings largely hold up against our data. But the deck conflates reach with quality — and our data provides the counterevidence.

What the Frame deck claims

  • Wildlife/science dominated — 54% of all PVs from one topic category
  • Celebrity stories averaged 8,039 PVs — highest per-story average
  • "Here are X takeaways" is the top replicable headline formula (avg 2,299 PVs)
  • "Here's a look" is underused (avg 5,214 PVs, 2 data points)
  • "What to know" performance is driven by subject specificity, not the CTA
  • Leading with a number outperforms trailing-number formats
  • High PVs → quality → ROI. Optimize for page views.

What our data adds or challenges

  • Headline formula findings confirmed — our CTR data extends and validates them
  • Possessive named entity construction (+142% CTR) not captured in Frame's analysis
  • Long headlines outperform short ones (+131% CTR) — counter to conventional wisdom
  • Local content on SmartNews outperforms all other categories by 7×
  • Active reading time is flat regardless of view count — views ≠ depth of engagement
  • Featured by Apple (6.7× view lift) actually produces shorter read times
  • Platforms don't share content — platform-specific optimization is necessary, not optional

The measurement problem the Frame deck doesn't address

The Frame deck's implicit argument is: high page views signal content quality, and content quality drives ROI. This is a reasonable prior — but our Apple News 2025 data, which includes active reading time alongside views, tests it directly and finds it doesn't hold.

The Frame deck measures demand. It tells you which article types and headline formulas attract the most clicks. That's genuinely useful. What it cannot tell you is whether readers who clicked stayed, read, saved, or came back — the signals that predict subscriber behavior and longer-term retention value.

Our data shows those two things — reach and depth — are statistically uncorrelated. Optimizing for PVs without accounting for engagement depth is optimizing half the equation.

Key Finding · Deep Dive

Views and reading depth are statistically independent populations

This is the finding that most directly challenges the "clicks = quality" framing. The Apple News 2025 dataset — 3,039 articles, full year — includes both Total Views and Avg. Active Time in seconds. This is rare: most distribution data gives you reach or depth, not both. We have both.

Three things that make this finding more precise

1. Featured by Apple boosts views 6.7× — and slightly reduces reading time

Articles surfaced by Apple's editorial algorithm receive dramatically more views than unfeatured content (median 10,911 vs. 1,619). But their median active time is 51 seconds — six seconds shorter than unfeatured articles (57s). This difference is statistically significant (Mann-Whitney p < 0.0001).

What this means: Apple's editorial promotion drives discovery, not depth. Readers who find an article because the algorithm surfaced it are less engaged on average than readers who sought it out. High reach ≠ high intent. This should inform how "Featured by Apple" articles are counted in any ROI model — they inflate views while slightly diluting engagement quality.

2. Subscribers read for less time than non-subscribers

On subscription-gated content, Apple News+ subscribers have a median active time of 49 seconds. Non-subscribers on free content read for 56 seconds. This gap likely reflects behavioral differences: subscribers are efficient, high-frequency readers who scan and move on; non-subscribers encountering a story organically may evaluate it more slowly. Neither is "wrong" — but they're different populations producing different depth signals.

3. Saves are the engagement metric that does track views

While active time is uncorrelated with views (r=−0.01), saves are strongly correlated (r=0.82). So are likes (r=0.77) and article shares (r=0.68). High-view articles do generate more social and save behavior — but not more reading depth. Saves are a proxy for intent to return, not evidence of a completed read. Both signals matter, but they measure different things.

What this means for the variant allocation model: View count alone is an incomplete ROI signal. The model should incorporate engagement depth (active time, saves) alongside reach — especially when comparing article types that may attract large audiences but low read completion vs. smaller but more-engaged audiences. A variant that drives 5,000 views with 80s average active time may deliver more long-term subscription value than one that drives 20,000 views with 45s.

Finding · Platform ROI by Content Type

Local and U.S. national content deliver outsized SmartNews ROI — Entertainment does not

The production/performance mismatch: Entertainment is the second-highest-volume category (13,713 articles, 36% of the dataset) and produces the lowest median per-article return (224 views). Local content is 3% of volume and produces 74× more views per article. This isn't a data curiosity — it's a signal about where variant production effort should concentrate on SmartNews specifically.

Platform ROI profiles differ enough to warrant separate variant strategies

What performs on SmartNews (local, civic, geographic specificity) is not what performs on Apple News push notifications (celebrity relationships, named-entity drama, escalating serial stories). MSN's top content in December 2025 was almost entirely national politics. These platforms are not interchangeable distribution channels — they have distinct audience expectations and algorithmic preferences.

Finding · Apple News Push CTR

Two headline features drive 2× CTR. One entire format consistently fails.

The Apple News Notifications sheet is the only dataset in either file with a direct headline-to-click signal: push notification text, impressions, and CTR for 351 notifications sent Jan–Feb 2026. Median CTR across all notifications: 1.7%. The spread is wide.

% CTR lift above or below baseline (1.7%) by headline feature. Apple News Notifications, Jan–Feb 2026. Features with n≥5 only.

What the top 10 highest-CTR notifications have in common

The top 10 are almost entirely one story: the Nancy Guthrie abduction and its connection to Savannah Guthrie. CTRs of 0.065–0.096 vs. a median of 0.017. Four to six times the baseline. The structural pattern across all ten:

Possessive named entity + escalating stakes + exclusive framing. "Savannah Guthrie's husband breaks silence…" — the possessive construction signals insider access and relationship proximity. Each notification in the series added a new development. Readers who clicked one installment clicked subsequent ones at even higher rates. This is a distinct content type: the serial escalating story with a celebrity anchor.

Finding · Cross-Platform Overlap

These platforms are separate content ecosystems — not distribution channels for the same content

% of each platform's articles that appear exclusively on that platform vs. also appearing on another. 2025, exact title match.

Of the roughly 43,000 unique articles across all four platforms, zero appear on all four. Only 32 appear on three or more. Pairwise overlaps are minimal relative to each platform's total volume. Apple News and MSN share only 35 exact titles. SmartNews, with 35,000+ articles, overlaps modestly with MSN (510) and Yahoo (346) — but that's still under 3% of its content.

The implication for variant strategy: Platforms are not seeing the same articles. This isn't a limitation — it's an opportunity. Variants written for SmartNews (local angle, geographic specificity, civic framing) should be different in emphasis and structure from variants written for Apple News push (named-entity drama, possessive construction, escalating stakes). A single "generic variant" deployed to all platforms is leaving platform-specific lift uncaptured.
